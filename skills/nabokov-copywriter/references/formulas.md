# Formulas — structure by goal

A formula is a running order for the reader's attention, not a template to fill.
Pick one from the goal (SKILL.md Step 0), then pour the *enlivened* material into
its shape. The copy stays specific to the user's facts; the formula only decides
what comes first.

## Choosing by goal

- **Sell** → **PAS** (short: post, ad, email) or **AIDA** (longer: landing page,
  long email). **ODC** when the audience is already warm and just needs a reason
  to act now.
- **Reach** → **BAB** (the after-state is shareable), or a bare **open loop**
  with a satisfying payoff.
- **Provoke** → **PMHS** (push the pain past comfortable), or drop the formula and
  state a real, defensible stance in the first line.
- **Trust** → **QUEST** for a long read, or plain grounded exposition — the macro
  pass in nabokov-editor's references, not a sales skeleton.

## The formulas

**PAS — Problem · Agitate · Solution.** Name a pain the reader already feels.
Agitate it: show the cost of leaving it unsolved (from real consequences, not
invented dread). Then the product as the way out. The workhorse for short copy.

**AIDA — Attention · Interest · Desire · Action.** A hook that stops the scroll.
Interest through what it does. Desire through the enlivened before/after and proof
(real testimonials, numbers, cases — from the user). A clear action to close.

**PMHS — Pain · More Pain · Hope · Solution.** PAS with the agitation extended,
then a sharp turn to relief. Provocative by design; use it when engagement matters
more than comfort. Don't manufacture pain the audience doesn't have.

**BAB — Before · After · Bridge.** Paint the reader's current world (Before).
Then paint the world with the problem gone (After); this is where sensory,
show-don't-tell rendering does the most work. Then the product is the bridge
across. Strong for outcomes people aspire to.

**ODC — Offer · Deadline · CTA.** Ultra-short, for a warm audience. The offer's
concrete value, a real deadline or scarcity (never a fake countdown), one
unambiguous ask. Nothing else.

**QUEST — Qualify · Understand · Educate · Stimulate · Transition.** For long,
trust-building pieces. Qualify: name who this is for (and, implicitly, who it
isn't). Understand: show you know their specific problem. Educate: teach something
they can use even if they never buy. Stimulate: connect the lesson to the product.
Transition: move to the ask.

## Open loops

Open a question the reader needs closed, then delay the answer. A headline that
withholds ("How losing our biggest client led to our best month") makes the reader
finish the piece to resolve it. Rules: the loop must pay off — an unanswered loop
reads as a bait headline and burns trust. One or two per piece; more turns the
copy into a tease. Open early, pay off before the CTA.

## CTAs

The call to action should grow out of the last line of copy, not arrive as a
bolted-on banner. It states one action, removes one risk (guarantee, free tier,
"cancel anytime" — only real ones), and where the goal is Sell, gives an honest
reason to act now. Match its force to the goal: Sell and Reach earn a direct ask;
Trust often closes better on a question or a next step than a hard sell.

## What was left out on purpose

The source research pushed region-specific SMM tactics (a fixed content ratio, a
"post in the local language for +N% reach" claim, "UGC converts N× better"). Those
numbers are unverified and locale-bound — kept out of this skill deliberately.
Add a localization reference only when a real, cited source backs it.
